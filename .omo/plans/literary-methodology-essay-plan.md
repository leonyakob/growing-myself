## C. Core thesis and subclaims

### Main thesis

This system's value is not that AI reads for the user. Its value is that it separates the things most easily swallowed by organization, analysis, and writing: first preserving the reading scene, then upgrading questions, then archiving with traceable boundaries, then consolidating the whole book, and only then compressing the material into writeable, refutable core tensions.

In shorter form:

> The workflow is not meant to mechanize reading. It is meant to make AI keep boundaries: when to preserve, when to analyze, when to archive, when to consolidate, and when to transform reading into writing.

### Subclaims

1. The starting point is not note-organization efficiency, but literary literacy training.
2. WeChat Reading preserves reading scenes better than ordinary notes, but it naturally fragments material.
3. Prompt optimization moved from adding rules to rebuilding the system because the tasks had become different cognitive actions.
4. The five stages are not a product flow; they are five reading actions with different failure modes.
5. Workflow gives different materials different homes: not every light touch becomes a main card, and not every card must become article material.
6. Stage 4 and Stage 5 are the key hinge: Stage 4 preserves complexity; Stage 5 accepts compression and selection pressure.
7. Anti-erasure requires label separation, speaker separation, original-text preservation, and strict boundaries around external reader comments.
8. QA/regression samples protect semantic accuracy, textual evidence, and literary energy rather than format.
9. The *Life* Stage 5 experiment shows that whole-book material can be compressed into writeable, refutable questions without flattening the user's original sharpness.

## D. Detailed article outline

### 1. Opening: the problem was never "how to organize", but "how to read more deeply"

- Question to answer: Why did this system begin from literary literacy training rather than tool efficiency?
- Key material: five-abnormalities scanning, question ladder, tension map, textual landing, "questions may jump far; proof cannot skip steps".
- Core judgment: literary literacy training begins at the place where the text feels abnormal, not at the place where the reader can state a conclusion.
- Possible examples: `索性`, `履历表`, `巧珍的爱情`, and the user's sharp line "他是农民时爱农民刘巧珍，他是干部时爱广播员黄亚萍".
- Most likely failure: writing abstract definitions instead of showing why these tools were needed.

### 2. WeChat Reading: it preserves the scene and produces fragments at the same time

- Question to answer: Why are WeChat Reading materials both precious and insufficient?
- Key material: highlights, user thoughts, colors, chapter/range positions, same-position high-like reader comments, round-based new material.
- Core judgment: WeChat Reading data is excellent for preserving the reading scene but does not automatically create article structure.
- Possible examples: a short feeling can remain a light card; an external high-like thought may echo/challenge the user but cannot become the user's judgment.
- Most likely failure: turning this section into a WeChat Reading feature explanation.

### 3. From rule patches to system reconstruction

- Question to answer: Why did prompt optimization have to shift from "add rules" to "rebuild stages"?
- Key material:
  - Do not overwrite user thoughts.
  - Do not turn external reader comments into user judgments.
  - Do not force light cards into deep analysis.
  - Do not migrate prematurely.
  - Do not rerun Stage 3 during whole-book consolidation.
  - Do not continue card supplementation during Stage 5 writing transformation.
- Core judgment: when tasks are different cognitive actions, adding more rules inside one undifferentiated prompt increases confusion; rules must return to their respective stages.
- Concrete conflict chain:
  - "only organize" vs evaluate;
  - problem-quality-only evaluation vs full-card rewriting;
  - optimize expression vs preserve original text;
  - migration/archive vs intermediate draft workspace;
  - whole-book consolidation vs local migration;
  - writing transformation vs continued card supplementation.
- Most likely failure: writing this as a prompt update chronology.

### 4. Five stages as five cognitive actions

- Question to answer: What does each stage protect, change, and forbid?
- Proposed stage explanation:
  1. Stage 1: preserve the reading scene. Worst error: premature analysis.
  2. Stage 2: deepen questions and judgments. Worst error: covering the user's original voice.
  3. Stage 3: archive into formal notes. Worst error: losing source evidence or replacing the user with AI revision.
  4. Stage 4: consolidate the whole book. Worst error: letting article index swallow card bodies.
  5. Stage 5: transform into writing. Worst error: slipping back into card supplementation or writing a decorative total judgment without evidence/counterevidence.
- Core judgment: stages exist so AI knows what it is allowed to do and what it must not do at that moment.
- Most likely failure: listing input/output mechanically without explaining the underlying reading action.

### 5. Turning literary reading into workflow means giving each material a proper home

- Question to answer: How can literary reading become a workflow without losing literary quality?
- Key material: light card/main card, detail card/theme card, four-part archive, reading-scene archive, article-material index, one-card-two-destinations.
- Core judgment: workflow does not replace reading; it gives materials with different literary weights different destinations.
- Possible examples:
  - Light cards preserve atmosphere and heart movement.
  - Main cards must develop evidence, questions, and writing direction.
  - A card can remain itself while also being referenced by an article index.
- Most likely failure: making workflow sound like technical optimization rather than boundary management.

### 6. Core tension: preserving reading scene vs producing article material

- Question to answer: Why is `reading-scene archive + article-material index` the key structure?
- Tensions to write through:
  - original feeling vs later analysis;
  - card body vs article index;
  - light touch vs main question;
  - literary expression vs textual evidence;
  - user judgment vs AI supplement.
- Core judgment: a good system does not push every material toward an article; it lets different materials have different futures.
- Possible example: *Life* Stage 4 first records the four-round reading progression, then provides article directions only as navigation.
- Most likely failure: writing the article-material index as if it were already an essay outline.

### 7. Preventing AI from erasing first reactions

- Question to answer: What exactly does AI tend to erase, and how does the system stop it?
- Key material:
  - label separation;
  - speaker/identity separation;
  - AI revision cannot cover user original text;
  - external reader comments cannot be disguised as user judgment;
  - lively user sentences should be preserved first;
  - light cards should not be forced into full cards;
  - full-card evaluation only applies to complete cards.
- Core judgment: AI's common failure is not inability to analyze; it is analyzing too quickly and turning a living reading scene into a neat answer.
- Possible example: the user's line about Gao Jialin loving Qiaozhen when a farmer and Huang Yaping when a cadre must be preserved for its sharpness, then supported with evidence rather than smoothed away.
- Most likely failure: making this an abstract ethics claim without concrete boundaries.

### 8. Semantic QA: protecting judgment, fact, and literary sharpness

- Question to answer: Why does a literary reading system need QA/regression samples?
- Key material: stage QA, pre-migration source check, post-migration anti-regression, Stage 4 fixtures, Stage 5 self-check, plan gate, Metis/Momus/Oracle review, regression sentinels.
- Core judgment: QA is not format checking; it prevents semantic regression, evidence substitution, factual drift, and flattening of literary energy.
- Required *Life* examples:
  - Gao Jialin refusing to dig coal cannot be reduced to "he had been a teacher"; the issue is labor, identity meaning, and dignity anxiety.
  - Huang Yaping's material giving cannot simply be dismissed as "not sacrifice"; it has real cost but differs from Qiaozhen's support under scarcity.
  - Disaster rescue arriving five hours late cannot be rewritten as "delayed five hours on the road".
  - A sharp/literary sentence should not be criticized merely because it is too neat; preserve it, then build the evidence bridge.
- Most likely failure: leaving QA in technical vocabulary instead of explaining it as semantic protection.

### 9. The *Life* experiment: why Stage 5 closes the loop

- Question to answer: Why did *Life* make Stage 5 necessary and convincing?
- Key material:
  - Stage 4 completed whole-book consolidation.
  - Stage 5 compressed the book into three questions.
  - Fixed chain: `安身失败 → 外部目光夺权 → 关系责任后撤 → 巧珍承担代价`.
  - Evidence-counterevidence tables, counterevidence-based revision, tension map, five-abnormalities close-reading points, writing paths, Lu Yao technique archive, cross-work linkage.
- Core judgment: *Life* Stage 5 is not proof that the prompt system is impressive; it is proof that clear boundaries can turn scattered reading reactions into writeable, refutable questions without erasing the original fire.
- Possible examples: `一个吻为什么会变成履历表`; `主语一换，责任就退了`; `同车不同心`.
- Most likely failure: writing a mini-literary essay on *Life* instead of using it as method evidence.

### 10. Ending: what this system ultimately trains

- Question to answer: What ability is this system really building?
- Abilities to name:
  - feeling preservation;
  - question upgrading;
  - textual close reading;
  - counterevidence handling;
  - tension structuring;
  - writing transformation;
  - author-understanding;
  - cross-work comparison.
- Costs and risks to admit:
  - prompts become thick;
  - QA costs are high;
  - regression samples require maintenance;
  - over-processing can suppress reading spirit.
- Core ending judgment: literary reading fears both chaos and premature order; this workflow tries to make order arrive later, more accurately, and without stealing the first feeling.
- Most likely failure: ending with a list rather than a final methodological judgment.
